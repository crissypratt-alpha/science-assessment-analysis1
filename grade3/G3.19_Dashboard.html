What Do the Complexity Levels Mean?

From Achieve's Framework to Evaluate the Cognitive Complexity of Science Assessment Tasks (2019).

Scripted (Recall)
Student recalls a fact or definition. No reasoning. DOK Level 1. Target: 0%.
Low Guided (Application)
Student applies a concept to a familiar scenario. Some reasoning. Target: 40-50%.
High Guided (Transfer)
Student applies understanding to unfamiliar situations with data/evidence. Target: 20-30%.
Doing Science (Authentic)
Genuine scientific reasoning with novel data. No single right path. Target: 5-10%.

Content Coverage Flags

Issues with construct redundancy and topic balance
  • ETS Severely Underrepresented (3% vs. 17% target)
    Only 1 ETS question (Q34 — race car track engineering). Despite being a Strong item, the domain needs 4-5 more questions. Use freed LS slots to add engineering design items with trade-off analysis.
  • LS Overrepresentation (50% vs. 44% target)
    17 of 34 questions are Life Science. While closer to target than many G3 tests, 3-4 LS items could be converted to ETS to improve balance. Four LS1-1 questions (Q1, Q2, Q21, Q22) all test life cycle recall and are redundant.
  • Four LS1-1 Life Cycle Recall Items (Q1, Q2, Q21, Q22)
    Four questions target the same standard (3-LS1-1) and all are Scripted recall about life cycle stages. This is significant redundancy. Consolidate into one or two data-driven life cycle questions to free slots for underrepresented domains.
  • Four PS2-3 Items All Below High Guided (Q16, Q17, Q18, Q19)
    Four questions target static electricity and magnetism but none reach High Guided. Q17 and Q18 are Scripted despite having scenarios. Consider consolidating into fewer, more complex investigation-based items.
  • Strong Claim-Evidence Pair (Q30-Q31: Horse Nettle Investigation)
    Q30 and Q31 form an excellent assessment pair where students first construct a claim from experimental data, then select supporting evidence. This two-question format demonstrates how to assess both claim construction and evidence evaluation. Use as a model for other standards.
  • Eight Strong Questions Demonstrate Effective Assessment Design
    Q11 (Pine Valley weather), Q12 (flood barrier engineering), Q23 (rabbit inheritance data), Q28 (Lakeview claim evaluation), Q30-Q31 (spine investigation), Q33 (Santiago climate), and Q34 (track engineering) all use real data and multi-step reasoning. These should serve as models for upgrading the 17 Scripted items.

Q1 — Complete Metamorphosis Diagram (Scripted → High Guided)
Replace visual recognition with evidence-based analysis.
Before: "Which diagram shows complete metamorphosis?"
After: "Scientists recorded the body changes of four unfamiliar insects over 10 weeks [data table showing dates, body length, number of legs, wing presence, and body form]. Based on the data, which insect undergoes complete metamorphosis? What evidence from the data supports your answer?"
Scripted → High Guided

Q2 — Pumpkin and Maple Tree Life Cycles (Scripted → High Guided)
Replace recall with comparative data analysis.
Before: "How are the life cycles of a pumpkin plant and a maple tree similar?"
After: "Students tracked the growth of three different plants over 12 months [data table showing seed germination date, first leaf date, flowering date, and seed production date for each]. What life cycle stages do all three share? What pattern do you see in the timing? Use data to support your answer."
Scripted → High Guided

Q4 — Parrot Inherited vs. Learned Traits (Scripted → High Guided)

After: "A scientist studied 20 parrots raised by different owners [data table showing beak color, feather pattern, vocabulary size, and trick ability for each parrot and its parents]. Which traits match the parents and which vary by owner? What pattern in the data helps you tell inherited traits from learned traits?"
Scripted → High Guided
