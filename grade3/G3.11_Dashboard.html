What Do the Complexity Levels Mean?

From Achieve's Framework to Evaluate the Cognitive Complexity of Science Assessment Tasks (2019).

Scripted (Recall)
Student recalls a fact or definition. No reasoning. DOK Level 1. Target: 0%.
Low Guided (Application)
Student applies a concept to a familiar scenario. Some reasoning. Target: 40-50%.
High Guided (Transfer)
Student applies understanding to unfamiliar situations with data/evidence. Target: 20-30%.
Doing Science (Authentic)
Genuine scientific reasoning with novel data. No single right path. Target: 5-10%.

Content Coverage Flags

Issues with construct redundancy and topic balance
  • Excessive LS Overrepresentation (60% vs. 44% target)
    18 of 30 questions test Life Science content. This crowds out ESS and ETS domains, both of which are significantly underrepresented.
  • ESS Underrepresentation (10% vs. 17% target)
    Only 3 ESS questions. Need at least 2 more to approach NGSS proportional representation.
  • ETS Underrepresentation (10% vs. 17% target)
    Only 3 ETS questions. Need at least 2 more engineering design items to reach target.
  • Redundant Recall Items in Same Content Area
    Q4 and Q19 both ask about animal groups at recall level. Q8 and Q3 both test magnet concepts at recall level. These redundancies waste assessment slots that could test different constructs or higher complexity.

MAP Alignment: Before & After Redesigns
1
Q2 — Butterfly Life Cycle Ordering (TEI)
Replace memorized sequence ordering with evidence-based life cycle analysis.
Before: “Put these stages in order.”
After: “A scientist observes an insect going through these changes [images of unfamiliar organism stages]. Based on the evidence, which life cycle pattern does this insect follow — complete or incomplete metamorphosis? Explain your reasoning.”
Scripted → High Guided
2
Q8 — Magnet Poles
Replace pure recall with data-driven pattern identification.
Before: “What happens when you bring the north pole close to the south pole?”
After: “Students tested different magnet orientations and recorded their results [data table]. Based on the data, what pattern do you see in how magnets interact?”
Scripted → High Guided
3
Q9 — Weather vs. Climate
Replace definition recall with data interpretation.
Before: “What’s the difference between weather and climate?”
After: “A student recorded the temperature every day for a year [graph]. Another student looked at 30 years of temperature data [graph]. Which student is studying weather, and which is studying climate? How does the data help you tell the difference?”
Scripted → High Guided
4
Q4 — Zebra Herds
Replace recall with evidence-based analysis.
Before: “How does living in a herd help zebras survive?”
After: “Scientists tracked predator attack success rates: solitary zebras were caught 35% of the time, while zebras in herds of 20+ were caught only 5% of the time. Based on this data, explain how herd size affects zebra survival.”
Scripted → High Guided
5
Q19 — Animal Groups
Replace with data analysis question.
Before: “Which of these animals typically lives in a large group?”
After: “Scientists observed these four animals for one month and recorded how often each was seen alone vs. in groups [data table]. Based on the data, which animals benefit from group living? How does the evidence support your answer?”
Scripted → High Guided
6
Q10 — Desert Plant Roots
Add investigation data.
Before: “Why do some plants in the desert have very long roots?”
After: “Scientists measured root lengths of plants in three environments: desert (avg 15m), grassland (avg 2m), and rainforest (avg 0.5m). Based on this data, what pattern do you see? What does this tell you about the relationship between root length and water availability?”
Scripted → High Guided
7
Q20 — Tree Frog Camouflage
Add experimental evidence.
Before: “How does being hard to see help the frog?”
After: “Researchers placed green and brown model frogs on green leaves and brown bark. After 24 hours, they counted how many were attacked by predators [data table]. Based on the results, explain how a frog’s color affects its survival in different environments.”
Scripted → High Guided
8
Q17 — Penguin Fat
Add comparative data.
Before: “How does a penguin’s thick layer of fat help it survive?”
After: “Scientists measured blubber thickness in three marine mammals [data table showing species, blubber thickness, and water temperature of habitat]. Based on this data, what pattern connects blubber thickness to the environment? How does this help explain penguin survival?”
Scripted → High Guided
9
Q23 — Cactus Spines
Add ecosystem interaction data.
Before: “How do the spines on a cactus help it survive in the desert?”
After: “Scientists compared two groups of cacti: one with spines intact and one with spines removed. After one month, 80% of spine-removed cacti showed animal bite damage vs. 5% of intact cacti. Based on this data, explain how spines help cacti survive.”
Scripted → High Guided
10
Q3 — Magnet Distance-Force
Add investigation data.
Before: “How does the force between two magnets change as you move them farther apart?”
After: “Students measured how many paper clips a magnet could pick up at different distances [data: 1cm=12 clips, 3cm=6 clips, 5cm=2 clips, 10cm=0 clips]. Based on this data, describe the pattern. Predict how many clips the magnet could pick up at 7cm.”
Scripted → Doing Science
